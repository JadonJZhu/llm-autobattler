While Large Language Models (LLMs) excel in single-turn interactions, they often struggle with multi-turn reasoning tasks that require gathering evidence over time. Existing benchmarks primarily focus on single-turn problems and are highly susceptible to memorization or test-set leakage. To address this gap, the authors introduce the Wason Inductive Logic Test (WILT), a multi-turn benchmark explicitly designed to resist memorization and evaluate how well models navigate continuous interactions without falling into repetitive failure patterns like the "doom loop".

WILT is inspired by the classic Wason 2-4-6 task and challenges models to uncover a hidden mathematical or logical rule (such as $x < y < z$). Models are given up to 30 turns to propose test cases consisting of three numbers, receiving a simple "True" or "False" response from the environment after each attempt. Because **every test begins with an identical starting state and clean slate, models cannot rely on pre-learned answers; instead, they must actively hypothesize, test, and adapt** based on the feedback they receive before submitting one final guess.

The benchmark reveals that **state-of-the-art LLMs struggle significantly with multi-turn inductive logic, with the highest-performing model (Claude 3.5 Sonnet) achieving an accuracy of only 28%**. Success on WILT requires two distinct capabilities: efficiently narrowing down an infinite hypothesis space by posing informative test cases, and demonstrating deductive reasoning to formulate the correct, simplest hidden function based on the collected evidence. Many models fail by either repeating test cases uselessly or succumbing to confirmation bias, where they repeatedly try to confirm a single hypothesis rather than attempting to falsify it.

Further experiments highlight that different LLMs exhibit distinct, non-overlapping strengths when handling these two core capabilities. The researchers conducted a test-swap experiment where models were asked to guess the hidden rule using the reasoning-stripped test cases generated by a different model. This revealed that **composing the test-generation abilities of one model with the deductive function-inversion capabilities of another often yielded better results than relying on a single top-tier model**, underscoring the multifaceted nature of multi-turn reasoning. Additionally, models varied widely in their adherence to Occam's Razor, with some consistently guessing simpler rules while others overfit the data with overly complex logic.

Ultimately, the paper concludes that **strong single-turn performance on traditional benchmarks does not guarantee multi-turn competence**. Typical single-turn benchmarks may actually incentivize models to end conversations quickly, which directly conflicts with real-world collaborative tasks—such as code debugging—that require continuous exploration, clarifying questions, and evidence gathering. WILT provides a more realistic measurement of reasoning by forcing models to navigate their own decisions, update their hypotheses, and learn from their mistakes across an extended conversation.
